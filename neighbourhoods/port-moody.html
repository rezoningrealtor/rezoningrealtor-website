About the City

Why Families Choose Port Moody

Port Moody is the Tri-Cities' most character-rich city — and arguably one of the most liveable small cities in all of Metro Vancouver. It sits at the head of the Burrard Inlet, giving it a harbour-town feeling that Coquitlam and Port Coquitlam simply can't replicate. The combination of walkable St. John Street, Rocky Point Park on the inlet, and two SkyTrain stations creates a lifestyle that attracts professionals, families, and creative types alike.

The city is well-known for its craft brewery scene — Brewers Row on Murray Street has made Port Moody a destination for the entire Lower Mainland, not just locals. But that same concentration of independent businesses, the Inlet Theatre, the arts centre, and the strong farmers market culture reflects a city that invests in its own quality of life. People who move to Port Moody tend to stay.

Port Moody is the smallest of the Tri-Cities but consistently punches above its weight for schools, amenities, and community engagement. It also extends into the Village of Anmore — a distinct municipality on the city's northern edge, known for its larger acreage properties and truly rural feel, drawing families who want something completely different from typical suburban living.

Who Lives Here

Community & Demographics

Port Moody consistently ranks among Metro Vancouver's most educated communities, with a high proportion of households with university degrees and professional occupations. The city attracts technology workers, healthcare professionals, educators, and creative professionals who value the lifestyle Port Moody offers over the closer-to-downtown alternatives in Burnaby or East Vancouver.

The city is more ethnically homogeneous than Coquitlam but has become increasingly diverse over the past decade, with growing South Asian, Chinese, and Korean communities — particularly in the Heritage Mountain and Heritage Woods areas. The cultural mix is reflected in the school system and community events.

There is a strong arts-and-outdoors identity here: the city attracts families who ski in winter, hike in summer, and care about local culture. The Inlet Theatre, Port Moody Arts Centre, and the public library are well-attended civic institutions. Community engagement is high — if you're moving here, expect neighbours who know each other by name.

Transportation

Getting Around Port Moody

Port Moody has excellent transit access for a city of its size, with two SkyTrain stations and a West Coast Express stop. The downtown area and SkyTrain stations are walkable from significant portions of the city. Heritage Mountain and Anmore are car-dependent, but most of the city proper has reasonable transit options.

🚇

SkyTrain — Moody Centre

Moody Centre station (Evergreen Extension) is the hub of Port Moody's downtown, adjacent to St. John Street. Direct connection to Coquitlam Central and Vancouver. ~45 min to Waterfront.

🚇

SkyTrain — Inlet Centre

Inlet Centre station connects the Suter Brook / Newport Village area. Walkable for residents of south Port Moody. Same Evergreen Extension as Moody Centre, one stop east toward Coquitlam.

🚂

West Coast Express

Port Moody station provides peak-hour express commuter rail to Waterfront in ~30 min. Excellent option for daily Vancouver commuters. Station is within the Moody Centre SkyTrain station.

🚗

Murray-Clarke / Hwy 7A

The Murray-Clarke Connector (Hwy 7A) provides direct car access east to Coquitlam and west to Burnaby and Vancouver. Heritage Mountain residents rely primarily on driving for daily needs.

Green Spaces

Parks & Trails

Port Moody's geography — sandwiched between the Burrard Inlet and forested mountain slopes — gives it an extraordinary park system for a city of ~35,000. Rocky Point Park alone would be enough to justify the city's reputation, but the Shoreline Trail, Bert Flinn Park, and nearby Belcarra Regional Park make this one of the best-served outdoor communities in Metro Vancouver.

  • Rocky Point Park Port Moody's crown jewel and one of the most beloved waterfront parks in the entire Lower Mainland. Sits right on the Burrard Inlet with a pier, beach, kayak launch, spray park, climbing structure, and walking paths. On summer evenings, the whole city seems to gather here. Dog-friendly, family-friendly, and stunningly beautiful at sunset.
  • Shoreline Trail A 10-kilometre trail running along the southern edge of Port Moody along the Burrard Inlet. Paved and unpaved sections wind through forest and along the water, connecting Rocky Point Park to Old Orchard Park. One of the most scenic urban trails in Metro Vancouver.
  • Bert Flinn Park A forested park in the Heritage Mountain area with trails ranging from easy walks to challenging hikes. Very popular with Heritage Mountain and Heritage Woods families who access it directly from their neighbourhoods. Dogs allowed off-leash in designated areas.
  • Belcarra Regional Park & Sasamat Lake A short drive from Port Moody, Belcarra is a Metro Vancouver Regional Park with beaches, trails, and the famous Sasamat Lake — consistently the warmest freshwater lake for swimming in the Lower Mainland. White Pine Beach is packed all summer. Effectively Port Moody's backyard lake.
  • Old Orchard Park A family park on the Port Moody–Port Coquitlam border with sports fields, a spray pad, playground, and picnic areas. Accessible via the Shoreline Trail. The western terminus of the PoCo Trail system as well, making it a trail connector between the two cities.
  • Seaview Park & Ioco Road Trails A local-favourite forest trail network along the north shore of the Burrard Inlet, connecting to the historic Ioco townsite. Less travelled than Rocky Point, but rewarding for families who want to explore off the beaten path.

Education

Schools in Port Moody

Port Moody is served by School District 43 (SD43) and has a strong reputation for its secondary schools. Both Port Moody Secondary and Heritage Woods Secondary are well-regarded, and the city's educated professional demographic tends to drive high parental engagement in school programs. French Immersion is available through SD43 at the elementary level.

Secondary · SD43

Port Moody Secondary

Serves Port Moody Centre, Suter Brook, and the lower areas of the city. One of SD43's larger secondaries. Strong IB Programme, arts, and athletics. Located near the SkyTrain corridor.

Neighbourhood Guide

Port Moody's Key Neighbourhoods

Despite being the smallest Tri-City, Port Moody has distinct neighbourhoods — each offering a very different lifestyle. Click each below to understand what makes it special for families.

Port Moody Centre

The walkable heart of Port Moody — where St. John Street, Brewers Row, Rocky Point Park, SkyTrain, and Suter Brook Village converge to create the most complete urban-meets-nature lifestyle in the Tri-Cities.

What Makes It Unique

  • St. John Street is a genuine Main Street — independent restaurants, cafés, boutiques, and services all walkable
  • Brewers Row on Murray St: multiple craft breweries (Moody Ales, Yellow Dog, Twin Sails, etc.) make PoMo a craft beer destination
  • Rocky Point Park is a 5–10 minute walk from most of the area — waterfront sunsets are part of daily life here
  • Moody Centre SkyTrain + West Coast Express both here — best transit access in all of Port Moody
  • Suter Brook Village and Newport Village: newer mixed-use developments with condos, cafés, grocery, and professional services
  • Inlet Theatre and Port Moody Arts Centre bring genuine cultural programming to the community

Good to Know for Families

  • Mix of condos/townhomes (Suter Brook) and detached homes (older PoMo streets) — something for every family type
  • Port Moody Secondary catchment — IB Programme available
  • Walkability score is exceptional by suburban Metro Vancouver standards
  • Higher price points for detached homes given the lifestyle premium
  • Shoreline Trail accessible from almost anywhere in the neighbourhood
  • Dog owners: one of the best neighbourhoods in the Tri-Cities — trails, parks, and dog-friendly patios everywhere

Heritage Mountain & Heritage Woods

Port Moody's elevated residential sanctuary — panoramic mountain and inlet views, trail access from doorstep, Heritage Woods Secondary, and a quiet neighbourhood character that attracts professional families who want space without sacrificing schools.

What Makes It Unique

  • Elevated terrain offers dramatic views of the Burrard Inlet, Indian Arm, and the North Shore mountains
  • Direct trail access to Bert Flinn Park — families hike directly from their neighbourhoods into old-growth forest
  • Heritage Woods Secondary is the catchment school — strong academic culture and high community engagement
  • Quiet, low-traffic streets with excellent safety for children walking to school
  • Newer homes (1990s–2010s) on generous lots — well-maintained with modern infrastructure
  • Heritage Mountain is slightly older and more established; Heritage Woods is the newer planned community east of it

Good to Know for Families

  • Car-dependent for most daily needs — drive to SkyTrain or Moody Centre takes 10–15 min
  • Premium price point; the view and school catchment command a measurable premium
  • Strong community culture — Heritage Mountain Community Association runs events year-round
  • Winter road conditions on elevated streets can be challenging during snow events (rare but happens)
  • Best Port Moody option for families who prioritise outdoor access and school quality equally
  • Belcarra and Sasamat Lake are a short drive — the "backyard" expands significantly with a car

Anmore

A separate village municipality tucked into the forested slopes north of Port Moody — BC's most rural community within Metro Vancouver, where acreage properties, equestrian estates, and old-growth trees define a lifestyle that's unlike anywhere else in the region.

What Makes It Unique

  • Anmore is its own municipality — with its own Village Council, very low density (1+ acre minimum lots in many areas), and a self-governing community character
  • Sasamat Lake is essentially the village's backyard — Belcarra Regional Park borders the community directly
  • Custom estate homes on large lots — properties here are distinctive and often architecturally unique
  • Horse properties and equestrian facilities are present — a rarity in Metro Vancouver
  • Exceptionally quiet and private — traffic noise is minimal and night skies are actually dark
  • Indian River Drive offers some of the most beautiful driving in the Lower Mainland — through old-growth along the creek

Good to Know for Families

  • Fully car-dependent — no transit, no commercial services within Anmore itself
  • Drive to Port Moody Centre (SkyTrain, schools, groceries) takes 15–20 min
  • Children typically attend Port Moody or Heritage Woods Secondary via SD43
  • Higher price point: acreage properties command significant premiums; entry-level is well above Tri-Cities average
  • Power outages during storms are more common than in Port Moody proper — generator ownership is common
  • Best fit for families who genuinely want rural BC living with a Metro Vancouver address
