About the City

Why Families Choose Port Coquitlam

Port Coquitlam — universally called PoCo by locals — is the smallest of the Tri-Cities by area but consistently ranks as one of the most liveable communities in Metro Vancouver for families. It has something that's genuinely rare: a real downtown. McAllister Avenue and the Leigh Square community hub give PoCo an actual town centre where people know each other, local businesses thrive, and community events happen on a human scale.

PoCo draws families who want a detached home with a real yard, good schools, and a community that still feels like a neighbourhood — without paying Coquitlam or Port Moody prices. The city has generally more affordable housing than its neighbours while sharing the same school district, the same trail system, and the same access to the Tri-Cities lifestyle.

The Traboulay PoCo Trail — a 25-kilometre paved loop around the entire city — is perhaps the clearest expression of PoCo's character: it was a community-led project built over decades, and it remains a source of enormous local pride. On a sunny weekend, it feels like the whole city is out on that trail.

Who Lives Here

Community & Demographics

Port Coquitlam has a historically blue-collar identity rooted in its working-class origins as a railway and industrial town — and while the workforce has shifted substantially toward professional and trades households, that community-over-status ethos has persisted. PoCo families tend to be deeply engaged in local sports, schools, and community organizations.

The city has become increasingly diverse over the past two decades. South Asian and Filipino communities have established strong presences, and the school district reflects this with growing multilingual programs. The population skews toward young and mid-career families who've made a deliberate choice for space and community over proximity to Vancouver.

PoCo is also notable for its arts community — the Terry Fox Library, Leigh Square, and the PoCo Heritage & Cultural Society maintain a genuine cultural scene for a city of its size. The birthplace of Terry Fox, PoCo holds that legacy with real pride, and the annual Terry Fox Run started here in 1981.

Transportation

Getting Around Port Coquitlam

PoCo is primarily a car-dependent city, but it has commuter rail access via the West Coast Express that makes downtown Vancouver commuting very viable during peak hours. The city's flat terrain and the PoCo Trail network also make cycling a practical option within the city.

🚂

West Coast Express

Port Coquitlam station provides express commuter rail to Waterfront in ~42 min. Peak-hour service only (morning eastbound, evening westbound). Excellent for Vancouver-bound commuters.

🚌

TransLink Bus to SkyTrain

Bus routes connect PoCo to Coquitlam Central SkyTrain station (Millennium Line) for non-peak transit. The trip to Coquitlam Central takes roughly 15–20 min by bus.

🚗

Lougheed Hwy / Hwy 7

Lougheed Highway (Hwy 7) runs through the city, providing direct car access to Coquitlam, Burnaby, and Vancouver. Golden Ears Bridge south connects to Pitt Meadows and Maple Ridge.

🚲

PoCo Trail (Cycling)

The 25km Traboulay PoCo Trail is paved and loops the entire city, connecting major parks and residential areas. Many residents commute within the city by bike using the trail.

Green Spaces

Parks & Trails

PoCo's trail network is its crown jewel. The Traboulay PoCo Trail system — built over decades through community effort — connects virtually every corner of the city on a paved, multi-use path. Combined with Colony Farm and the Pitt River dyke trails, PoCo offers more trail variety per resident than almost anywhere in Metro Vancouver.

  • Traboulay PoCo Trail The city's defining feature — a 25-kilometre paved loop encircling Port Coquitlam along rivers, dykes, and forest edges. Ideal for cycling, jogging, and dog walking. Locals use sections of it daily; doing the full loop is a PoCo rite of passage.
  • Colony Farm Regional Park A flat, open regional park shared with Coquitlam, spanning both sides of the Coquitlam River. Exceptional for birdwatching (great blue herons, eagles, waterfowl) and peaceful nature walks. A completely different character from the forested trails elsewhere.
  • Gates Park A large active-use park in the Riverwood neighbourhood with sports fields, ball diamonds, a spray park for kids, and event space. One of the most-used parks in the city, especially on summer weekends when families from Fremont gather here.
  • Leigh Square & Lions Park Leigh Square is PoCo's civic plaza — a gathering space for farmers markets, concerts, and community events in the heart of downtown. Lions Park adjacent to the civic core is a pleasant green space within walking distance of the West Coast Express station.
  • Pitt River Dyke Trails Accessible from the south end of PoCo, the Pitt River dyke offers flat, wide trail walking with views across the Pitt Meadows delta and Golden Ears. A favourite for families with strollers and cyclists wanting long, flat routes.
  • Birchland Manor Trail System A network of forested trails in the northwest of PoCo, connecting into the Coquitlam trail system. More natural and less manicured than the PoCo Trail — popular with mountain bikers and trail runners.

Education

Schools in Port Coquitlam

Port Coquitlam is served by School District 43 (SD43), the same district as Coquitlam and Port Moody. The city has strong secondary schools and a well-regarded Catholic independent school option. French Immersion is available at the elementary level through SD43.

Secondary · SD43

Terry Fox Secondary

Named for Port Coquitlam's most famous son — Terry Fox was born and grew up in PoCo. Strong academic and athletic programs. Serves central and north PoCo neighbourhoods.

Neighbourhood Guide

Port Coquitlam's Key Neighbourhoods

PoCo is made up of a range of neighbourhoods, each with its own character, home styles, and everyday lifestyle. Explore below to see which one fits you best.

Central PoCo / Downtown

The civic and commercial heart of Port Coquitlam — a walkable downtown with community events, independent businesses, the West Coast Express station, and a genuine neighbourhood feel that's rare in Metro Vancouver.

What Makes It Unique

  • McAllister Ave is a real working main street with local shops, restaurants, and services - not a strip mall
  • Leigh Square: civic plaza hosting farmers markets, live music, and seasonal events — the community gathers here
  • West Coast Express: PoCo station is walking distance from central neighbourhoods — downtown Vancouver in ~42 min
  • New Port Coquitlam Community Centre alongisde Terry Fox Library and Leigh Square Arts Centre bring genuine cultural infrastructure to the area
  • Home to the annual PoCo Grand Prix cycling race — streets close for a real competitive cycling event
  • Gates Park is the neighbourhood's anchor — sports fields, splash park, and event spaces all within walking distance

Good to Know for Families

  • Mix of older detached homes (1970s to 1990s), newer infill, mostly in form of newer townhomes and low storey apartments
  • Walkable to almost everything PoCo has to offer — rare for a Tri-Cities community
  • More affordable entry point than Riverwood or Oxford Heights
  • Active community association and neighbourhood programs throughout the year
  • Mostly flat terrain — cycling and walking to school is practical for kids
  • Riverside Secondary catchment — active school community

Riverwood (Fremont)

Port Coquitlam's master-planned south end — a newer, spacious neighbourhood on the Pitt River flats where families find larger homes, great parks, and a community built around outdoor living.

What Makes It Unique

  • Fremont Village anchors the area with shops, groceries, cafes, and everyday essentials — everything you need is within minutes
  • Nature is built into daily life with Traboulay PoCo Trail, Blakeburn Lagoon, and Minnekhada Regional Park all close by
  • Big-box convenience nearby — Costco, Walmart, Home Depot, and more without long drives
  • Quiet, family-oriented streets with a thoughtful mix of townhomes and detached homes
  • Pitt River dykes and trail systems offer flat, scenic routes for biking, jogging, and weekend walks
  • Well-connected location with quick access to Pitt River Bridge, Coquitlam Centre, and SkyTrain

Good to Know for Families

  • Primarily newer development compared to older PoCo neighbourhoods — popular with young families and upsizers
  • Strong access to schools including Terry Fox Secondary School and nearby elementary options
  • Higher price point than central PoCo but still more affordable than comparable Coquitlam
  • More suburban feel compared to Downtown PoCo — quieter, less foot traffic, more space
  • Excellent for outdoor-oriented families who want nature and convenience in one place
  • Flooding history in low-lying areas — worth checking specific addresses near the river

Citadel Heights

A quiet, elevated neighbourhood known for its space, views, and sense of privacy.

What Makes It Unique

  • Elevated hillside setting with scenic Fraser River and mountain views — one of the few PoCo pockets with real elevation
  • Quiet, tucked-away streets with minimal through traffic — feels private compared to most of Port Coquitlam
  • Strong connection to nature with trails, greenbelts, and parks integrated into the neighbourhood
  • Primarily detached homes on larger lots, and larger townhomes offering more space, and a true suburban feel
  • Quick access to Mary Hill Bypass and Highway 1 — ideal for commuters heading to Burnaby, Surrey, or beyond
  • A hidden pocket that feels removed from the city while still being minutes to daily essentials

Good to Know for Families

  • Popular with upsizers and long-term homeowners looking for space and stability
  • School catchments include Citadel Middle School and Riverside Secondary School
  • Larger homes and lot sizes compared to Riverwood, Glenwood, or Central PoCo
  • Less walkable for errands — most trips are a short drive
  • Hilly terrain — great for views, but something to consider for day-to-day walkability
  • Smaller inventory as compared to other areas and so homes here tend to be tightly held and less frequently available

North Port Coquitlam (Woodland Acres, Glenwood, Oxford Heights)

Right Next to Coquitlam Centre… without Paying for It.

What Makes It Unique

  • Mostly closer to Coquitlam Centre with full access to shopping, dining, and transit without paying Coquitlam pricing
  • Close to Coquitlam skytrain stations that offer easier connection to Downtown Vancouver
  • Steps from Lafarge Lake and Town Centre Park — one of the best lifestyle hubs in the tri-Cities
  • Larger lots with more established, tree-lined streets that feel quieter and more lived-in than newer master-planned areas
  • Everyday convenience along Lougheed Hwy and Coast Meridian — groceries, services, and essentials all nearby
  • Strategic location with quick access to Coquitlam, Port Moody, and Highway 1 corridors

Good to Know for Families

  • Mix of older detached homes (1960s to 1980s), and increasing infill redevelopment - duplexes & triplexes
  • More attainable entry point compared to Coquitlam Centre and Burke Mountain
  • Strong school access including Riverside Secondary School and Terry Fox Secondary School
  • Highly practical for commuters — transit + driving options in all directions
  • Less dense, more space — ideal for families wanting room without leaving central locations
  • Consistent demand from both end-users and investors due to location and long-term upside
